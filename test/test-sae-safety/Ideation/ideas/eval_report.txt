================================================================================
SYNTHESIZED ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

KEY STRENGTHS (consensus):
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Well-structured and accessible presentation. All three reviewers acknowledge
   the paper is clearly written at a high level (Clarity scores 6-7). The
   problem motivation -- improving mechanistic interpretability tools for AI
   safety monitoring -- is well-articulated and timely.

2. Ambitious integration of multiple relevant techniques. The proposal brings
   together contrastive learning, sparse autoencoders, hierarchical circuit
   analysis, and online feature steering into a unified framework. The breadth
   of the vision is commendable.

3. Hierarchical Circuit SAE is the most novel component. Reviewer 1 explicitly
   identified this as the most original contribution. The idea of composing
   token-level SAE features into circuit-level representations for safety
   monitoring has genuine conceptual appeal.

4. Addresses a real and important problem. AI safety monitoring through
   mechanistic interpretability is a high-value research direction. If the
   proposed framework worked as described, the impact would be meaningful.


KEY WEAKNESSES (consensus):
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Contrastive loss formulation is mathematically inconsistent with stated
   goals. Reviewer 1 identified that the numerator of the contrastive loss
   pulls safe and unsafe representations together rather than apart. Reviewer 3
   independently noted that contrastive and reconstruction losses may conflict.
   This is a fundamental technical flaw that undermines the core training
   objective.

2. Critical implementation details are undefined. All three reviewers flag
   missing specifications:
   - The iterative labeling procedure for safe/unsafe pairs (R1, R2)
   - The importance weighting scheme (R1, R3)
   - The steering calibration mechanism (R3)
   These are not minor details -- they are load-bearing components of the
   proposed system.

3. MMCS evaluation metric assumes nonexistent ground-truth safety features.
   Both Reviewer 2 and Reviewer 3 independently identify this as a validity
   concern. There is no established ground truth for what a "safety feature"
   should look like in a neural network's latent space, making the proposed
   primary evaluation metric circular.

4. Scalability of hierarchical SAE is unaddressed. The 512K+ dimensional input
   to the hierarchical stage (R1, R3) raises serious computational and
   optimization concerns. Gradient starvation in such high-dimensional sparse
   regimes is a real risk (R1), and no mitigation strategy is proposed.

5. Limited novelty -- individual components are well-established. All reviewers
   converge on the assessment that the contribution is primarily an engineering
   integration of existing techniques (contrastive learning, SAEs, RepE-style
   steering) rather than a methodologically novel advance. Reviewer 2
   specifically notes the steering module closely resembles existing
   Representation Engineering work.

6. Insufficient justification over simpler baselines. Reviewer 2 raises the
   important question of why SAE-based approaches are needed when simpler tools
   (linear probes, RepE, input/output filters) already exist and are well-
   validated. No compelling argument or empirical comparison is provided.


UNIQUE INSIGHTS:
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

- Reviewer 1 (Senior ML Researcher): Identified a subtle circularity between
  the steering mechanism and the assumption of monosemantic features -- steering
  presupposes the features are already well-disentangled, but the contrastive
  objective is supposed to achieve that disentanglement.

- Reviewer 2 (Domain Expert): Noted that HH-RLHF covers only a narrow slice of
  safety-relevant behaviors. A safety monitoring system evaluated only on
  helpfulness/harmlessness dialogues would have unknown generalization to other
  safety failure modes (deception, power-seeking, sycophancy, etc.).

- Reviewer 3 (Methods Specialist): Flagged that 10M tokens is likely
  insufficient training data for an 8B-parameter model's feature space. Standard
  SAE training in the literature uses significantly larger corpora.


ACTIONABLE SUGGESTIONS:
---------------------------------------------------------------------------

1. Fix the contrastive loss formulation. The current formulation must be
   corrected so that safe and unsafe feature representations are pushed apart,
   not pulled together. Consider adopting a supervised contrastive loss
   (Khosla et al., 2020) with safety labels as the grouping variable, or a
   triplet loss with explicit safe-anchor, safe-positive, unsafe-negative
   structure.

2. Fully specify the iterative labeling pipeline. Provide a concrete algorithm
   with stopping criteria, quality assurance steps, and analysis of label noise
   propagation across iterations. Without this, the approach cannot be
   reproduced or evaluated.

3. Replace MMCS with a grounded evaluation metric. Consider using downstream
   task performance (e.g., safety classifier accuracy using discovered features,
   attack success rate reduction with steering) as the primary metric rather
   than an ungrounded feature-matching score.

4. Provide theoretical or empirical analysis of when SafeSAE outperforms
   simpler baselines. Run head-to-head comparisons against linear probes, RepE,
   and standard SAEs on the same safety benchmarks. If the additional complexity
   does not yield clear gains, the contribution is difficult to justify.

5. Address scalability with concrete experiments or analysis. Provide at minimum
   a computational budget analysis and ideally preliminary results on the
   hierarchical SAE at scale, including training stability diagnostics.

6. Expand evaluation beyond HH-RLHF. Include diverse safety benchmarks
   (TruthfulQA, AdvBench, XSTest, or custom adversarial evaluations) to
   demonstrate generalization of the safety monitoring capability.

7. Increase training data significantly. Scale from 10M tokens to at least
   100M-1B tokens to be commensurate with the model size and feature space
   complexity.

================================================================================
FINAL DECISION
================================================================================

Decision: borderline_accept

Rationale: The overall average score of 5.73 falls in the borderline_accept
range (>= 5.0 and < 6.0). While all three reviewers independently recommended
borderline reject, the research direction is timely and important, the
hierarchical Circuit SAE component offers genuine novelty, and the identified
weaknesses -- particularly the contrastive loss formulation error and missing
implementation details -- are addressable with revision rather than
fundamentally fatal. The unanimous agreement on low validity (5.0) is the most
concerning signal, but it stems primarily from fixable technical issues rather
than a flawed research premise.

Action: The paper should undergo a major revision addressing the six actionable
suggestions above before resubmission. The authors should prioritize (1) fixing
the contrastive loss formulation, (2) specifying the iterative labeling
procedure, and (3) providing empirical comparisons against simpler baselines.
A revised version that addresses these core issues could move into solid accept
territory. The authors are encouraged to resubmit after revision.
