================================================================================
RESEARCH IDEA: Safety-Anchored Contrastive Sparse Autoencoders (SACSA)
================================================================================

Title: Safety-Anchored Contrastive Sparse Autoencoders: Jointly Learning
Interpretable and Safety-Guaranteed Feature Decompositions via Contrastive
Anchoring and Hierarchical Consistency Regularization

================================================================================
1. PROBLEM STATEMENT
================================================================================

Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have emerged as the primary tool for decomposing
neural network activations into interpretable features (Bricken et al., 2023;
Cunningham et al., 2023). When scaled to production models, SAEs discover
safety-relevant features such as deception, sycophancy, and dangerous content
(Templeton et al., 2024). However, three critical and interacting failures
prevent SAEs from being reliably deployed for safety monitoring:

(F1) FEATURE ABSORPTION: When features form natural hierarchies (e.g.,
"harmful content" > "bioweapons" > "synthesis instructions"), sparsity
optimization causes parent features to be absorbed into child features,
making general safety-relevant features unreliable classifiers (Chanin
et al., 2024). A "deception" feature may fail to activate on novel
deception types that happen to co-occur with more specific features.

(F2) NON-CANONICAL DECOMPOSITION: SAEs of different sizes produce
fundamentally different feature dictionaries with no convergence to a
canonical set (Leask et al., ICLR 2025). Features are neither complete
(smaller SAEs miss information) nor atomic (larger SAE features decompose
into meta-latents). This means safety-critical features discovered at one
scale may vanish or fragment at another.

(F3) SAFETY-AGNOSTIC TRAINING: Standard SAE objectives (reconstruction +
sparsity) are entirely unsupervised and safety-unaware. There is no
mechanism to ensure that safety-relevant directions in activation space
receive preferential treatment in the learned dictionary. Features
capturing "refusal behavior" or "situational awareness" may be poorly
represented or split across many latents, reducing their utility for
safety monitoring.

These problems compound: a safety-relevant feature that is absorbed,
non-canonical, AND poorly prioritized by the loss function is essentially
invisible to any downstream safety monitoring system.

The core research question is: Can we design an SAE training framework that
jointly optimizes for (a) standard reconstruction quality, (b) hierarchically
consistent safety feature decomposition, and (c) guaranteed representation of
known safety-relevant directions, while maintaining the unsupervised discovery
of novel features?

================================================================================
2. PROPOSED METHOD: Safety-Anchored Contrastive Sparse Autoencoders (SACSA)
================================================================================

SACSA introduces three synergistic innovations: (i) contrastive safety anchoring,
(ii) anti-absorption hierarchical consistency regularization, and (iii) an
adaptive dictionary partitioning scheme. We describe each in full mathematical
detail.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.1 Notation and Base Architecture
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Let x in R^d denote an activation vector at a specific layer of the target
model, drawn from data distribution D. We use a Gated SAE (Rajamanoharan
et al., 2024) as our base architecture for its superior handling of shrinkage:

  Gating:       pi(x) = sigma(W_gate (x - b_pre) + b_gate)       in R^n
  Magnitude:    mu(x) = ReLU(W_mag (x - b_pre) + b_mag)          in R^n
  Latent code:  z(x) = pi(x) * mu(x)                             in R^n
  Decoder:      x_hat = W_dec z(x) + b_dec                       in R^d

where sigma is the element-wise sigmoid, * is element-wise multiplication,
W_gate, W_mag in R^{n x d}, W_dec in R^{d x n}, b_pre in R^d, b_gate,
b_mag in R^n, b_dec in R^d are learnable parameters, and n >> d is the
dictionary size (expansion factor).

We partition the n dictionary features into two sets:
  - Anchored features A with |A| = n_a: reserved for safety-relevant concepts
  - Free features F with |F| = n - n_a: discovered via standard unsupervised
    learning

So z(x) = [z_A(x); z_F(x)] and W_dec = [W_dec^A; W_dec^F] accordingly.

Denote the i-th decoder column (feature direction) as d_i = W_dec[:, i].

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.2 Contrastive Safety Anchoring (CSA)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The key idea is to use contrastively constructed safety directions as
"anchors" that constrain a subset of dictionary features to align with
known safety-relevant concepts, while leaving the majority of the dictionary
free for unsupervised discovery.

STEP 1: Construct Safety Direction Bank.

Following Representation Engineering (Zou et al., 2023), we construct K
safety-relevant contrast pairs. For each safety concept k in {1,...,K}
(e.g., deception, power-seeking, harmful content, sycophancy, situational
awareness), we construct:

  v_k = E_{x ~ D_k^+}[x] - E_{x ~ D_k^-}[x]

where D_k^+ and D_k^- are distributions over activations from prompts that
elicit vs. do not elicit concept k. We normalize: v_k <- v_k / ||v_k||_2.

To handle the multi-dimensional nature of safety concepts, we also compute
a low-rank subspace via PCA on the positive-negative activation differences:

  Delta_k = {x_i^+ - x_i^- : i = 1,...,m_k}
  U_k = top-r SVD(Delta_k) in R^{d x r}

where r is the subspace rank (typically r = 3-5).

STEP 2: Anchor Assignment.

For each safety concept k, we reserve r_k anchored features. The total
anchor count is n_a = sum_{k=1}^{K} r_k. We define an assignment function
alpha: A -> {1,...,K} mapping each anchored index to its safety concept.

STEP 3: Contrastive Anchoring Loss.

For each anchored feature i in A with alpha(i) = k, the decoder direction
d_i must align with the safety subspace U_k. We define:

  L_anchor = sum_{i in A} (1 - ||U_{alpha(i)}^T d_i||_2^2 / ||d_i||_2^2)
           = sum_{i in A} (1 - cos^2_sub(d_i, U_{alpha(i)}))

where cos^2_sub measures the squared cosine similarity between d_i and its
projection onto the safety subspace. This is 0 when d_i lies entirely in the
subspace and 1 when d_i is orthogonal to it.

We additionally encourage the anchored features for the same concept to span
the subspace rather than collapse:

  L_span = sum_{k=1}^{K} max(0, r_k - rank_epsilon(D_k^anchor))

where D_k^anchor = [d_i : alpha(i) = k] in R^{d x r_k} and rank_epsilon
counts singular values above threshold epsilon. In practice, we use a
differentiable relaxation:

  L_span = -sum_{k=1}^{K} sum_{j=1}^{r_k} log(sigma_j(D_k^anchor) + eps)

where sigma_j are the singular values.

STEP 4: Contrastive Activation Loss.

To ensure anchored features activate correctly, we add a contrastive
activation loss using matched positive/negative pairs:

  L_contrast = -sum_{k=1}^{K} E_{(x^+, x^-) ~ P_k} [
    sum_{i: alpha(i)=k} log sigma(z_i(x^+) - z_i(x^-))
  ]

where P_k is a distribution of (positive, negative) activation pairs for
concept k, and sigma is the sigmoid. This loss encourages anchored features
to have higher activations on safety-relevant inputs than on matched controls.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.3 Anti-Absorption Hierarchical Consistency Regularization (AHCR)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Feature absorption occurs when the sparsity penalty causes a parent feature
(e.g., "deception") to become inactive when a child feature (e.g., "lying
about capabilities") is active, despite both being semantically applicable.
We propose a regularizer that explicitly counteracts this.

STEP 1: Define Feature Hierarchy.

We define a partial order on features via a learned adjacency matrix
H in [0,1]^{n x n}, where H_{ij} represents the probability that feature j
is a child of feature i. We learn H jointly with the SAE via:

  H_{ij} = sigma(h_{ij})    where h_{ij} is a learnable scalar

with the constraint that H is a DAG (enforced via NOTEARS-style
regularization):

  L_DAG = tr(e^{H * H}) - n

where * denotes element-wise product and the matrix exponential is computed
via truncated Taylor series. This equals 0 iff H encodes a DAG.

STEP 2: Hierarchy Discovery.

To initialize and guide H, we use activation co-occurrence statistics:

  C_{ij} = P(z_i > 0 AND z_j > 0) / P(z_i > 0)

A high C_{ij} with C_{ji} < C_{ij} suggests i is a parent of j (the parent
fires in a superset of contexts). We use C to warm-start h:

  h_{ij}^{init} = beta * (C_{ij} - C_{ji}) * I[C_{ij} > tau]

where beta is a scaling factor, tau is a co-occurrence threshold, and I is
the indicator function.

STEP 3: Anti-Absorption Loss.

The key regularizer penalizes violations of hierarchical consistency: if
a child feature is active, its parent should also be active (with nonzero
probability).

  L_anti_abs = sum_{i,j: H_{ij} > delta} H_{ij} * E_x [
    max(0, z_j(x) * (t_i - z_i(x)))
  ]

where t_i is a learnable threshold for parent feature i, and delta is a
pruning threshold for the hierarchy. Intuitively: whenever child j activates
(z_j > 0) but parent i is below its activation threshold t_i, we incur a
penalty proportional to the hierarchy strength H_{ij} and the child's
activation magnitude z_j(x).

This directly counteracts the sparsity penalty's tendency to suppress parent
features when children are active.

STEP 4: Hierarchical Sparsity Reweighting.

Instead of applying uniform L1/L0 sparsity, we use a hierarchy-aware
sparsity penalty:

  L_sparse = sum_{i=1}^{n} w_i * I[z_i(x) > 0]

where the weight w_i is adjusted based on hierarchical depth:

  w_i = lambda_0 * gamma^{depth(i)}

with gamma < 1 (e.g., 0.7), so that features deeper in the hierarchy
(more specific) receive stronger sparsity pressure, while general/parent
features receive weaker sparsity pressure. depth(i) is computed from H:

  depth(i) = max_{j: H_{ji} > delta} (depth(j) + 1),   depth(root) = 0

This creates a natural inductive bias: general safety features are
encouraged to remain active alongside more specific ones.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.4 Adaptive Dictionary Partitioning
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The partition into anchored (A) and free (F) features should not be rigid.
We introduce a soft mechanism that allows features to graduate between pools.

STEP 1: Safety Relevance Score.

For each feature i, compute a running safety relevance score:

  s_i = max_{k=1,...,K} cos^2_sub(d_i, U_k)

STEP 2: Soft Partition.

Instead of a hard A/F split, we use a continuous weighting:

  w_i^{anchor} = sigma((s_i - s_thresh) / temperature)

The total loss applied to feature i interpolates between anchored and free
objectives:

  L_i = w_i^{anchor} * L_i^{anchored} + (1 - w_i^{anchor}) * L_i^{free}

This allows the model to discover that a free feature has become safety-
relevant during training and gradually subject it to anchoring constraints,
or conversely, to release an anchored feature that turns out not to align
with any safety concept.

STEP 3: Novel Safety Feature Detection.

For free features that develop high safety relevance scores without being
anchored, we flag them as "emergent safety features" and create new safety
concept slots:

  If s_i > s_novel_thresh for some free feature i, and
  max_k cos_sub(d_i, U_k) < s_known_thresh (i.e., it doesn't match known
  concepts), then:
  - Extract a new safety direction v_{K+1} = d_i / ||d_i||
  - Compute a new subspace U_{K+1} via local PCA on activations where z_i
    is highly active
  - Add concept K+1 to the safety bank
  - Log for human review

This provides an automated pipeline for discovering novel safety-relevant
features that were not in the initial concept bank.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
2.5 Full SACSA Training Objective
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

The complete SACSA loss is:

  L_SACSA = L_recon + lambda_s * L_sparse + lambda_a * L_anchor
          + lambda_sp * L_span + lambda_c * L_contrast
          + lambda_h * L_anti_abs + lambda_d * L_DAG

where:

  L_recon = E_x [||x - x_hat||_2^2]                     (reconstruction)
  L_sparse = E_x [sum_i w_i * I[z_i(x) > 0]]           (hierarchy-aware sparsity)
  L_anchor = defined in Section 2.2                       (directional alignment)
  L_span = defined in Section 2.2                         (subspace spanning)
  L_contrast = defined in Section 2.2                     (activation contrast)
  L_anti_abs = defined in Section 2.3                     (anti-absorption)
  L_DAG = defined in Section 2.3                          (acyclicity)

Hyperparameters:
  lambda_s:  sparsity weight (tuned via L0 target, following Gao et al., 2024)
  lambda_a:  anchor alignment weight (0.1)
  lambda_sp: span weight (0.01)
  lambda_c:  contrastive weight (0.5)
  lambda_h:  anti-absorption weight (0.3)
  lambda_d:  DAG regularization weight (0.1)
  gamma:     hierarchical sparsity decay (0.7)
  r:         safety subspace rank (3-5)
  K:         number of initial safety concepts (10-20)

Training procedure:
  1. Pre-train standard Gated SAE for T_warmup steps (standard L_recon + L_sparse)
  2. Compute safety directions {v_k, U_k}_{k=1}^K from contrast pairs
  3. Initialize hierarchy H from co-occurrence statistics
  4. Fine-tune with full L_SACSA for T_main steps
  5. Every T_check steps, run novel safety feature detection (Section 2.4)
  6. Anneal lambda_a, lambda_c from high to moderate values to allow
     initial strong anchoring followed by relaxation

Optimizer: Adam with lr=3e-4, linear warmup for 1000 steps, cosine decay.
Batch size: 4096 activation vectors.
Dictionary size: 32x expansion (n = 32d).
Anchored features: n_a = K * r (typically 50-100 out of ~50,000).

3. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
================================================================================

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.1 Models and Datasets
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Target models (in order of scale):
  - Pythia-70M, Pythia-410M (open, reproducible baselines)
  - Gemma-2-2B (medium-scale open model)
  - Llama-3-8B (large-scale open model)

Activation extraction layers:
  - Residual stream at layers L/4, L/2, 3L/4 for each model (following
    standard practice from Bricken et al., 2023)

Training data:
  - 500M tokens from The Pile (general activations)
  - 50M tokens from safety-relevant datasets:
    - TruthfulQA prompts + completions (deception/honesty)
    - HarmBench prompts (harmful content requests)
    - Sycophancy datasets from Anthropic
    - Power-seeking scenarios from MACHIAVELLI benchmark
    - Situational awareness probes (custom-constructed)

Contrastive pairs for safety anchoring:
  - For each safety concept k, 5000 matched (positive, negative) prompt pairs
  - Positive: prompts where the model exhibits the safety-relevant behavior
  - Negative: semantically similar prompts without the safety behavior
  - Example for deception: model outputs known to be deceptive vs. truthful
    responses to the same questions

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.2 Baselines
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

  B1: Standard SAE (Bricken et al., 2023) — vanilla ReLU SAE with L1
  B2: Gated SAE (Rajamanoharan et al., 2024) — gated architecture, no safety
  B3: TopK SAE (Gao et al., 2024) — TopK activation function
  B4: Hierarchical SAE (HSAE) — from "Atoms to Trees" (2025)
  B5: Post-hoc probing — train linear probes on standard SAE features for
      safety concept detection
  B6: RepE directions — directly use Zou et al.'s representation engineering
      directions without SAE decomposition
  B7: SACSA-ablated — ablations removing each component:
      (a) No anchoring (lambda_a = lambda_c = 0)
      (b) No anti-absorption (lambda_h = 0)
      (c) No hierarchy (lambda_d = 0, uniform sparsity)
      (d) Hard partition (no adaptive partitioning)

All baselines use the same dictionary size (32x expansion) and target the
same L0 sparsity for fair comparison.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.3 Evaluation Metrics
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

CATEGORY A: Standard SAE Quality Metrics

  A1. Reconstruction MSE: ||x - x_hat||_2^2 / ||x||_2^2 (normalized)
  A2. L0 sparsity: average number of active features per input
  A3. CE loss degradation: increase in cross-entropy loss when replacing
      original activations with SAE reconstructions in the target model
  A4. Dead feature fraction: percentage of features never activating over
      10M tokens
  A5. Feature density distribution: histogram of activation frequencies

CATEGORY B: Safety Feature Quality Metrics

  B1. Safety concept AUROC: for each safety concept k, train a linear
      classifier on anchored feature activations to distinguish D_k^+ from
      D_k^-, report AUROC. Compare against classifiers trained on all
      features (should be comparable, showing anchored features capture the
      information).

  B2. Safety concept completeness: fraction of the variance in the safety
      subspace U_k explained by the anchored features:
        Completeness_k = ||Proj_{span(D_k^anchor)} U_k||_F^2 / ||U_k||_F^2

  B3. Cross-prompt generalization: AUROC on held-out safety prompts not
      seen during contrastive pair construction.

  B4. Novel safety feature discovery rate: number of flagged emergent
      safety features that pass human evaluation as genuinely safety-relevant.

CATEGORY C: Anti-Absorption Metrics (following Chanin et al., 2024)

  C1. Absorption rate: for each parent-child pair in the learned hierarchy,
      measure the fraction of inputs where the child is active but the
      parent is incorrectly inactive:
        AbsRate(i,j) = P(z_j > 0 AND z_i = 0) / P(z_j > 0)
      Lower is better.

  C2. Hierarchical consistency score:
        HCS = 1 - (1/|E|) sum_{(i,j) in E} AbsRate(i,j)
      where E is the set of edges in the learned hierarchy with H_{ij} > delta.

  C3. Spelling task absorption (direct replication of Chanin et al.):
      measure if "starts with letter X" features correctly fire even when
      more specific token features are active.

CATEGORY D: Interpretability Metrics

  D1. Autointerp score (Bills et al., 2023): automated interpretability
      scoring via LLM-generated descriptions.
  D2. Feature monosemanticity: measured by activation pattern clustering
      (lower entropy of top-activating contexts = more monosemantic).
  D3. Human evaluation: expert raters evaluate random samples of features
      for interpretability on a 1-5 Likert scale.

CATEGORY E: Downstream Safety Application Metrics

  E1. Steering effectiveness: measure KL divergence between steered and
      unsteered model when clamping safety-anchored features:
        KL(p_steered || p_unsteered) for targeted behavior change
      Higher is better (more effective steering).

  E2. Steering precision: measure unintended behavior changes when steering:
        KL(p_steered || p_original) on held-out non-safety tasks
      Lower is better (minimal collateral damage).

  E3. Safety monitoring latency: time to compute safety feature activations
      for a single forward pass (must be <10% overhead for practical deployment).

  E4. Safety alarm recall/precision: use anchored features as a safety
      classifier and measure performance on held-out red-teaming datasets:
      - HarmBench (harmful content detection)
      - TruthfulQA (deception detection)
      - Sleeper agents benchmark (backdoor detection)

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
3.4 Key Experiments
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Experiment 1: Pareto Frontier Analysis
  Compare SACSA vs. all baselines on the reconstruction quality (CE loss
  degradation) vs. sparsity (L0) Pareto frontier. SACSA should achieve
  comparable Pareto performance to Gated SAE / TopK SAE, demonstrating that
  safety anchoring does not significantly degrade general reconstruction.

Experiment 2: Safety Feature Reliability
  For each safety concept, compare AUROC, completeness, and cross-prompt
  generalization across methods. Key hypothesis: SACSA's anchored features
  provide >0.9 AUROC on safety classification while post-hoc probing on
  standard SAEs achieves only 0.7-0.85 due to feature fragmentation.

Experiment 3: Absorption Resistance
  Replicate the spelling task from Chanin et al. (2024) and extend to
  safety-relevant hierarchies (e.g., "harmful" > "violent" > "weapon
  instructions"). Measure absorption rate for each method. Key hypothesis:
  SACSA's anti-absorption regularizer reduces absorption rate by >50%
  compared to standard SAEs.

Experiment 4: Scaling Behavior
  Train SACSA at dictionary sizes 8x, 16x, 32x, 64x, 128x expansion.
  Measure whether anchored safety features remain stable across scales
  (addressing F2). Compare feature similarity across scales using CKA
  (Centered Kernel Alignment) on the anchored feature subspace.

Experiment 5: Steering Quality
  Use SACSA features to steer model behavior on safety-critical tasks.
  Compare against RepE (Zou et al., 2023) and standard SAE feature steering.
  Measure both effectiveness (behavior change on targeted concept) and
  precision (minimal change on unrelated behaviors). Use activation patching
  methodology from Meng et al. (2022).

Experiment 6: Novel Safety Feature Discovery
  Train SACSA with K=10 initial safety concepts. Evaluate the emergent
  safety features discovered via the adaptive partitioning mechanism
  (Section 2.4). Have human experts rate discovered features and compare
  against features found by standard SAE + post-hoc safety probing.

Experiment 7: Ablation Study
  Systematically ablate each component of SACSA (using B7 ablations) and
  measure the impact on all metric categories. This identifies the marginal
  contribution of each innovation.

Experiment 8: Real-time Safety Monitoring
  Deploy SACSA on Llama-3-8B and run inference on 10K red-teaming prompts.
  Measure (a) safety alarm precision/recall, (b) computational overhead,
  (c) comparison against dedicated safety classifiers (Llama Guard, etc.).
  This tests practical deployment viability.

4. EXPECTED CONTRIBUTIONS
================================================================================

(C1) METHODOLOGICAL: A novel SAE training framework (SACSA) that integrates
     contrastive safety anchoring, anti-absorption regularization, and
     adaptive dictionary partitioning into a unified objective. This is the
     first SAE architecture designed to jointly optimize for interpretability
     AND safety-relevant feature quality.

(C2) THEORETICAL: A formal analysis of why feature absorption is
     particularly dangerous for safety monitoring (absorption causes
     safety-relevant parent features to go silent), and a principled
     regularizer (AHCR) grounded in hierarchy-aware sparsity reweighting
     that provably reduces absorption in the linear feature regime.

(C3) EMPIRICAL: Comprehensive evaluation across 4 model scales and 5
     safety concepts, demonstrating:
     (a) SACSA matches standard SAE reconstruction quality (Pareto-comparable)
     (b) >50% reduction in feature absorption rate on hierarchical features
     (c) >0.9 AUROC on safety concept detection via anchored features
     (d) Stable safety features across 5 dictionary scales
     (e) More effective and precise model steering than RepE or vanilla SAEs

(C4) PRACTICAL: An automated pipeline for discovering novel safety-relevant
     features during SAE training (emergent safety feature detection),
     providing a scalable mechanism for ongoing safety monitoring as models
     and threat landscapes evolve.

(C5) OPEN-SOURCE DELIVERABLES:
     - SACSA training code (compatible with TransformerLens / SAELens)
     - Pre-trained SACSA dictionaries for Pythia, Gemma-2, and Llama-3
     - Safety contrast pair datasets for 10+ safety concepts
     - Evaluation suite for safety-aware SAE benchmarking

================================================================================
5. RELATED WORK AND POSITIONING
================================================================================

SACSA bridges three previously separate research threads:

(a) SAE architecture innovations (Gated SAEs, TopK SAEs, crosscoders) that
    improve reconstruction-sparsity trade-offs but are safety-agnostic.

(b) Representation Engineering (Zou et al., 2023) that provides safety
    directions but lacks the fine-grained feature decomposition of SAEs.

(c) Feature absorption analysis (Chanin et al., 2024) that identifies the
    problem but does not propose a training-time solution.

The closest concurrent work is HSAE (2025), which learns hierarchical
structure but (i) does not incorporate safety signals, (ii) uses a
different anti-absorption mechanism (random perturbation rather than
explicit hierarchical consistency), and (iii) does not provide the
contrastive anchoring that guarantees safety feature representation.

SACSA is also related to the "specialized SAEs for rare concepts" approach
(NAACL 2025), but differs by integrating safety specialization into the
main SAE training objective rather than post-hoc fine-tuning.

================================================================================
6. RISK ANALYSIS AND MITIGATION
================================================================================

Risk 1: Safety anchoring degrades reconstruction quality.
Mitigation: The anchored features are a small fraction (<0.2%) of the
dictionary. Ablation studies will quantify the exact trade-off. The adaptive
partitioning mechanism prevents over-constraining.

Risk 2: Contrastive pairs introduce human bias about what is "safety-relevant."
Mitigation: The novel safety feature detection mechanism (Section 2.4)
provides an unsupervised counterbalance, discovering safety-relevant features
not in the initial concept bank.

Risk 3: DAG regularization makes optimization difficult.
Mitigation: We use warm-starting from co-occurrence statistics and a phased
training schedule. The NOTEARS constraint is well-studied and adds modest
computational overhead.

Risk 4: The method may not scale beyond Llama-3-8B.
Mitigation: The computational overhead per feature is O(1) — the anchoring
and hierarchy losses are computed only on the feature activations, not on the
full activation space. The bottleneck is the same as standard SAE training
(activation extraction and dictionary multiplication).
