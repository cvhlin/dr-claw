### Disagreement Resolution

No dimension exhibits a spread greater than 3 points. The maximum spread across
all dimensions is 1 point, occurring only on Significance (P1: 7, P2: 7, P3: 6).

The minor disagreement on Significance is well-justified by the reviewers'
differing focus areas. P3 (Methods Specialist) tempered the significance score
on the grounds that the practical impact is contingent on whether the learned
DAGs are semantically meaningful at scale -- noting that if edges turn out to be
optimization artifacts, the method "reduces to a slightly more expensive Gated
SAE with no interpretability gain." P1 and P2 assign Significance 7 because
they weight the potential upside more heavily: the safety applications, the
workflow improvement for practitioners (O(d+edges) DAG traversal replacing
O(d^2) patching), and the elegance of unifying three problems. This is a
legitimate difference in perspective rather than a factual disagreement, and the
averaged score of 6.67 appropriately reflects the conditional nature of the
contribution's significance. No override is needed.

The remarkable unanimity across all three reviewers (identical scores on 4 of 5
dimensions) reflects genuine consensus rather than anchoring: each reviewer
independently identified the same core strengths and weaknesses despite
approaching from distinct vantage points (theoretical validity, domain impact,
engineering feasibility).

### Synthesized Assessment

**Strengths (Consensus)**

1. Architecturally Elegant Core Idea. All three reviewers highlight the
   (I - A)^{-1} SEM layer as a genuinely novel contribution to the
   interpretability literature. No existing SAE variant jointly learns both a
   dictionary and a causal DAG over dictionary elements. The formulation is
   minimal -- a single structural modification to the latent space -- yet it
   simultaneously addresses feature absorption, circuit discovery, and
   principled intervention. This is the kind of unifying insight that, if
   validated, could reshape the SAE research agenda.

2. Well-Articulated Problem Decomposition. The three sub-problems (P1: feature
   absorption, P2: circuit blindness, P3: superposition ambiguity) are cleanly
   formulated with precise mathematical statements. Reviewers uniformly praise
   the clarity of exposition (all scoring 8/10), noting the numbered equations,
   explicit algorithmic descriptions, and the near-executable quality of the
   implementation plan.

3. Comprehensive Experimental Design. The proposal includes strong baselines
   (particularly B5: post-hoc NOTEARS on pre-trained SAE, which isolates the
   value of joint training), a synthetic benchmark with known ground truth,
   real-model experiments on established benchmarks, and extensive ablations.
   The experimental design reflects a mature understanding of what evidence is
   needed to validate the claims.

4. Intellectual Honesty. The explicit risk assessment with specific mitigations
   (Section 6) is noted by all reviewers as a sign of research maturity. The
   proposal does not shy away from identifying its own weak points.

**Weaknesses (Consensus)**

1. NOTEARS Scalability Is the Critical Technical Risk. All three reviewers flag
   this as the primary feasibility concern. The matrix exponential
   tr(exp(A * A)) is ill-conditioned for d > 1000, and the proposed dictionary
   sizes reach d = 65,536. The block-sparse approximation mitigates storage but
   introduces arbitrary restrictions on discoverable structure and adds
   implementation complexity that is underspecified. P1 and P3 both recommend
   DAGMA (log-determinant formulation) as a more numerically stable alternative.
   The augmented Lagrangian optimization is known to be fragile, and convergence
   to h(A) < 1e-8 while jointly training the SAE is a hard multi-objective
   problem that the warm-start strategy alone does not resolve.

2. Proposition 1 Is Too Narrow. The anti-absorption guarantee is restricted to
   a two-feature linear case with Bernoulli activations. All reviewers agree
   this is too simple to be convincing as a formal contribution. The
   generalization to thousands of features in superposition with nonlinear
   gating is a large and unaddressed gap. The proposition should either be
   extended to a more general setting (e.g., k features with bounded
   collinearity and maximum in-degree constraints) or explicitly labeled as
   illustrative rather than a formal guarantee.

3. "Causal" Claims Exceed the Evidence. The method learns from observational
   data (neural network activations). P2 articulates this most precisely:
   statistical dependencies can masquerade as causal edges, and the NOTEARS
   framework is sensitive to confounders. P1 raises identifiability conditions
   (faithfulness, sufficiency) that are not discussed. The consistency-with-
   activation-patching metric (C2) tests correlation, not causation, creating a
   fundamental validation gap. The proposal should distinguish between "causal"
   in the SEM structural sense and "causal" in the interventionist sense.

4. Hyperparameter Complexity. Five loss terms with associated weights, augmented
   Lagrangian scheduling parameters, topological reordering frequency, and
   block-sparse structure parameters create a tuning surface of at least 10
   dimensions. While ablations are planned, interaction effects are not studied.
   This raises reproducibility concerns.

5. Overambitious Experimental Scope. All reviewers independently conclude that
   the full experimental plan (7 experiments, 7 ablations, 3 models, multiple
   dictionary sizes, human evaluations) is 2-3x what is realistic for a single
   publication. The 120 A100-hour estimate covers training only, not the
   extensive evaluation suite.

**Unique Insights by Reviewer**

- P1 (Senior ML Researcher) uniquely raises the identifiability discussion:
  under what conditions on the data-generating process can the DAG be recovered?
  What happens with latent confounders (features the SAE fails to learn)? This
  is a foundational concern that the proposal must address.

- P2 (Domain Expert) provides the sharpest critique of the validation strategy:
  the synthetic benchmark uses the same SEM data-generating process the model
  assumes, creating "circular validation." P2 also makes the important safety
  point that unreliable causal tools in safety-critical contexts can be worse
  than no tools at all.

- P3 (Methods Specialist) offers the most actionable engineering suggestion:
  replacing the sequential forward substitution loop with a parallel message-
  passing formulation using batched sparse matrix-vector products, which would
  be GPU-friendly and address the overhead concerns during training.

**Suggestions for Improvement (Prioritized Synthesis)**

1. REDUCE SCOPE IMMEDIATELY. Start with GPT-2 Small at d = 4,096 (no block-
   sparse needed). Focus exclusively on (a) synthetic DAG recovery and (b) the
   spelling-task absorption benchmark. This alone is a compelling paper if
   results are strong.

2. REPLACE NOTEARS WITH DAGMA. The log-determinant acyclicity constraint is
   more numerically stable at high dimensions and avoids the matrix exponential.
   Benchmark both constraints on the synthetic task to justify the choice.

3. REFRAME CAUSAL LANGUAGE. Use "dependency-structured" or "causally-inspired"
   when referring to the learned DAG in the absence of interventional
   validation. Reserve "causal" for claims backed by do-interventions on learned
   features with verified downstream propagation.

4. STRENGTHEN PROPOSITION 1. Extend to a k-feature setting under bounded
   condition number and maximum in-degree assumptions, or clearly label the
   current version as "illustrative" rather than "formal guarantee."

5. ADD INTERVENTIONAL VALIDATION. After training, perform actual do-
   interventions on learned features and verify downstream responses match DAG
   predictions. This is the strongest possible evidence for the learned
   structure's validity.

6. IMPLEMENT GPU-FRIENDLY SEM LAYER. Replace the sequential topological-order
   loop with parallel fixed-point iteration via batched sparse matrix-vector
   products. Include wall-clock benchmarks comparing both implementations.

7. PROVIDE DAGMA/NOTEARS CONVERGENCE FALLBACK. Specify what happens if the
   acyclicity constraint does not converge: soft penalty fallback, alternative
   constraint, or early stopping criteria.

### Final Decision

**Decision: ACCEPT (Conditional)**

**Overall Score: 6.53 / 10**

**Rationale:**

The Causal-SAE proposal presents a genuinely novel architectural idea -- the
(I - A)^{-1} SEM layer embedded in the SAE latent space -- that elegantly
unifies three recognized problems in mechanistic interpretability: feature
absorption, circuit blindness, and intervention ambiguity. All three reviewers
independently recognize this as a meaningful contribution, and the proposal's
clarity and comprehensiveness are uniformly praised.

The overall average of 6.53 places this in the "accept" range (>= 6.0). The
strong scores on Clarity (8.0) and Novelty (7.0) reflect the genuine quality of
the core idea and its exposition. Feasibility (6.0) and Significance (6.67) are
solidly in the accept range, reflecting a realistic but achievable research
program with meaningful potential impact. Validity (5.0) is the weakest
dimension, sitting at the borderline_accept threshold, and reflects the
legitimate concerns about NOTEARS scalability, the narrow scope of the formal
guarantee, and the epistemological gap between observational training and causal
claims.

The decision is "accept" rather than "strong accept" because the validity
concerns are substantive and the gap between theoretical claims and available
evidence is real. However, the concerns are addressable: DAGMA can replace
NOTEARS, the proposition can be extended or reframed, the causal language can be
tempered, and the scope can be reduced to a focused, publishable core. None of
the weaknesses are fatal to the idea itself.

**Required Actions Before Proceeding to Full Implementation:**

(a) Adopt DAGMA as the primary acyclicity constraint; benchmark against NOTEARS
    on the synthetic task to validate the choice.

(b) Scope the initial experiments to GPT-2 Small (d = 4,096) with synthetic DAG
    recovery and the spelling-task absorption benchmark as the two primary
    evaluations.

(c) Rewrite all "causal" claims to distinguish structural (SEM) causality from
    interventionist causality, and add an identifiability discussion.

(d) Extend or reframe Proposition 1 before claiming a formal anti-absorption
    guarantee.

(e) Implement the SEM layer with GPU-friendly parallel sparse operations from
    the start.

If these five actions are taken, the resulting paper would be positioned as a
strong contribution to a top interpretability venue (ICML, NeurIPS, or ICLR
workshop track, with potential for the main conference if empirical results are
convincing).
